UNIT 1

Political Ideologies

Major "isms", their meanings, and basic ideological comparisons

What is an Ideology?

An ideology is a set of beliefs, values, and ideas that form the basis of a political, economic, or social system. It provides a framework through which individuals and societies understand and organize their world.

Key Point: Every political system is grounded in an ideology — whether explicitly stated or implicitly practiced.

Capitalism

An economic and political system where private individuals or businesses own the means of production. Markets and prices are determined by supply and demand. Key features include private property rights, free markets, and profit motive.

Socialism

An ideology advocating collective or government ownership and administration of the means of production and distribution. It aims for equality and social welfare. Ranges from democratic socialism to Marxist socialism.

Communism

A classless society where the means of production are owned communally. Associated with Marx and Lenin. In practice: the state controls all means of production with the goal of eventual stateless, classless society.

Fascism

An authoritarian ultra-nationalist ideology characterized by dictatorial power, forcible suppression of opposition, and rigid socioeconomic control. Examples: Nazi Germany, Mussolini's Italy.

Liberalism

A political ideology centered on individual rights, freedoms, rule of law, democracy, and limited government. Two major strands: classical liberalism (emphasizes individual liberty) and social liberalism (emphasizes social justice).

Conservatism

An ideology that promotes traditional institutions, customs, and values, and skepticism toward rapid change. It favors gradual reform over revolutionary change.

Nationalism

The belief that a nation should be self-governed. Nations are defined by shared culture, language, history, or ethnicity. Can be civic or ethnic nationalism.

Islamism / Islamic Ideology

For Pakistan, Islamic ideology refers to the belief that Islam should guide all aspects of life including politics, law, and governance. Pakistan's founding was based on the Two-Nation Theory — that Muslims of the subcontinent are a separate nation requiring their own homeland.

The Objectives Resolution (1949) declared that sovereignty belongs to Allah and that the state of Pakistan would be organized in accordance with Islamic principles.

Ideological Comparisons

Understanding key differences helps in exam comparisons:

  • Capitalism vs Socialism: Private vs collective ownership; market vs planned economy; profit-driven vs welfare-driven
  • Liberalism vs Conservatism: Change vs tradition; individual rights vs community stability
  • Communism vs Fascism: Both authoritarian, but communism is internationalist and leftist while fascism is nationalist and rightist
  • Nationalism vs Cosmopolitanism: National sovereignty vs global unity and cooperation

Sample Long Questions & Model Answers

Q1 What is ideology? Discuss the major political ideologies of the world with their key characteristics.
Introduction: An ideology is a comprehensive set of beliefs, values, and principles that guide how a society should be organized politically, economically, and socially. Every state operates according to some ideology, whether acknowledged or not.

Major Political Ideologies:

1. Capitalism: Based on private ownership of production, free market competition, and profit motive. Associated with Western democracies. The state plays a minimal role in the economy. Advocates argue it promotes innovation and efficiency; critics say it leads to inequality.

2. Socialism: Advocates collective ownership and redistribution of wealth for social welfare. The state plays an active role in managing the economy. Examples include Scandinavian social democracies.

3. Communism: A Marxist ideology calling for abolition of private property and class distinctions. The Communist Manifesto (1848) by Marx and Engels laid its theoretical foundation. Soviet Union and China adopted it in the 20th century.

4. Liberalism: Prioritizes individual freedom, democratic governance, and rule of law. Classical liberalism opposes government interference; social liberalism endorses welfare programs to ensure genuine freedom for all.

5. Conservatism: Values tradition, stability, and incremental change. Opposes radical reforms. Edmund Burke is considered its founding philosopher.

Conclusion: Understanding these ideologies is crucial as they shape the policies, constitutions, and international relations of states. Pakistan, founded on Islamic ideology, combines elements of Islamic principles with modern democratic governance.
Q2 Compare and contrast capitalism and socialism. Which system is more suitable for a developing country like Pakistan? Discuss.
Introduction: Capitalism and socialism represent two fundamentally different approaches to organizing an economy and society. Their comparison is central to understanding global politics and development economics.

Capitalism:
• Private ownership of means of production
• Prices determined by market forces
• Competition drives efficiency and innovation
• Inequality is an accepted outcome
• USA, UK, Germany are examples

Socialism:
• State or collective ownership of key industries
• Government plans and distributes resources
• Focus on equality and social welfare
• Can reduce innovation incentive
• Cuba, Nordic mixed economies are examples

Key Differences:
| Aspect | Capitalism | Socialism |
| Ownership | Private | Public/Collective |
| Goal | Profit | Social Welfare |
| Economy | Market-driven | Planned/Mixed |

For Pakistan: A mixed economy approach — combining market forces with state regulation and social programs — is most practical. Pakistan's Constitution reflects this balance: it allows private enterprise while ensuring state responsibility for social welfare (Article 38).

Conclusion: No ideology is perfectly applicable in isolation. Developing countries benefit most from pragmatic hybrid systems that take the strengths of both while addressing local realities.
Q3 What is the ideological basis of Pakistan? Explain the Two-Nation Theory and its significance.
Introduction: Pakistan came into existence on August 14, 1947, based on the Two-Nation Theory (TNT) — the idea that Hindus and Muslims of the Indian subcontinent were two distinct nations who could not coexist in a single unified state.

Two-Nation Theory: The TNT holds that religion forms the basis of nationhood. Muslims have their own culture, history, laws (Sharia), language traditions, and way of life that are fundamentally different from those of Hindus. Therefore, they deserve a separate sovereign state.

Key Proponents:
• Sir Syed Ahmad Khan — First articulated the idea of Muslim separateness
• Allama Iqbal — Provided its philosophical foundation (Allahabad Address, 1930)
• Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah — Translated it into political reality

Lahore Resolution (1940): The Muslim League formally demanded separate states for Muslims in the northwestern and eastern zones of India, which eventually became Pakistan.

Significance:
• Provided the ideological justification for Pakistan's creation
• Shaped Pakistan's Islamic constitution and legal system
• Continues to influence Pakistan's foreign policy, particularly toward India
• Guides national education and cultural identity

Conclusion: The Two-Nation Theory remains the foundational ideology of Pakistan, embedded in its Constitution, national narrative, and state identity.
UNIT 2

Constitution of Pakistan

Constitution-making, comparison of 1956, 1962, 1973, federalism and system of government

Constitution-Making Process

Pakistan's constitution-making was a long and difficult journey. The first Constituent Assembly (1947–1954) was tasked with both making a constitution and functioning as the legislature.

Objectives Resolution (March 12, 1949): Pakistan's first major constitutional document, declaring that sovereignty belongs to Allah and the state will exercise authority as His trustee. It became the preamble of all subsequent constitutions.

Constitution of 1956

Pakistan's first constitution, enacted on March 23, 1956. Pakistan became an Islamic Republic. Key features:

  • Federal parliamentary system of government
  • Unicameral legislature (National Assembly)
  • Islam declared state religion
  • Adult franchise for elections
  • Joint electorate system
  • Abrogated by Ayub Khan in 1958

Constitution of 1962

Ayub Khan's constitution, introduced a presidential system. Key features:

  • Presidential form of government — President had vast powers
  • Basic Democracies system (indirect elections through 80,000 Basic Democrats)
  • One National Assembly (156 seats)
  • No fundamental rights at first (added later)
  • Name changed from "Islamic Republic" to just "Republic" (restored in 1963)
  • Abrogated by Yahya Khan in 1969

Constitution of 1973

Pakistan's current constitution, passed unanimously on April 10, 1973. Drafted under Z.A. Bhutto. Key features:

  • Federal parliamentary system of government
  • Bicameral legislature: National Assembly + Senate
  • Islam declared state religion; Quran and Sunnah as supreme law
  • Fundamental rights guaranteed
  • Strong Prime Minister as head of government
  • President as constitutional head
  • Independent judiciary
  • Has been amended 26+ times
The 1973 Constitution is the only one unanimously passed by all parliamentary parties, giving it unique democratic legitimacy.

Comparison of Constitutions

Quick comparison table for exam:

  • System: 1956 — Parliamentary | 1962 — Presidential | 1973 — Parliamentary
  • Legislature: 1956 — Unicameral | 1962 — Unicameral | 1973 — Bicameral
  • Elections: 1956 — Direct | 1962 — Indirect | 1973 — Direct
  • Islam: All three declared Islam as state religion
  • Duration: 1956 — 2 years | 1962 — 7 years | 1973 — still in force

Federalism in Pakistan

Pakistan is a federal state where powers are divided between the federal government and the four provinces (Punjab, Sindh, KPK, Balochistan). The 1973 Constitution establishes three legislative lists:

  • Federal Legislative List (Part I): Exclusive federal jurisdiction (defence, foreign affairs, currency)
  • Federal Legislative List (Part II): Both federal and Council of Common Interests
  • Concurrent List: Abolished by 18th Amendment (2010), transferring subjects to provinces

Sample Long Questions & Model Answers

Q1 Compare the constitutions of 1956, 1962, and 1973. Which constitution best served Pakistan's interests?
Introduction: Pakistan has had three constitutions in its history — 1956, 1962, and 1973. Each reflected the political realities of its era and attempted to solve different governance challenges.

Constitution of 1956: Pakistan's first constitution established a parliamentary federal democracy with Islam as the state religion. However, it was abrogated within two years due to political instability and Ayub Khan's military coup in 1958. Its key weakness was its failure to resolve the East-West Pakistan political tension and ensure stable governance.

Constitution of 1962: Ayub Khan's constitution replaced parliamentary democracy with a presidential system and indirect elections (Basic Democracies). While it provided short-term stability, it was fundamentally undemocratic and concentrated power in the President. It failed to address regional grievances, contributing to the political crisis of 1969 and the eventual separation of East Pakistan.

Constitution of 1973: The most successful constitution, passed unanimously. It restored parliamentary democracy, created a bicameral legislature (protecting smaller provinces through the Senate), and entrenched fundamental rights. Though frequently suspended or distorted through amendments, its basic structure remains Pakistan's governing document today.

Best for Pakistan: The 1973 Constitution, despite its imperfections, best serves Pakistan's interests because: (1) it was passed with democratic consensus; (2) it protects provincial rights; (3) it balances Islamic principles with modern governance; (4) it provides a framework for civilian rule.

Conclusion: The 1973 Constitution represents Pakistan's most mature and inclusive constitutional experiment, and its survival over 50+ years (with amendments) confirms its fundamental suitability.
Q2 Explain the salient features of the 1973 Constitution of Pakistan.
Introduction: The 1973 Constitution, passed on April 10, 1973 under Prime Minister Z.A. Bhutto, is Pakistan's third and current constitution. It was the first to be passed unanimously by all parties.

Key Features:

1. Islamic Provisions: Islam is the state religion. The President and PM must be Muslim. Quran and Sunnah are declared supreme law. The Council of Islamic Ideology advises on legislation.

2. Federal System: Power is distributed between the federal government and four provinces. The Senate protects smaller provinces by giving equal representation.

3. Parliamentary System: The Prime Minister is the head of government with real executive power. The President is a constitutional figurehead. The government is accountable to parliament through a vote of no-confidence.

4. Bicameral Legislature: National Assembly (lower house, 342 seats, elected directly) and Senate (upper house, 100 seats, representing provinces equally).

5. Fundamental Rights: Chapter 1 guarantees rights including equality before law, freedom of expression, religion, assembly, and movement. Suspended during martial law but restored.

6. Independent Judiciary: Supreme Court, High Courts, and subordinate courts. Judicial review of legislation. Judges appointed through a judicial commission.

7. Emergency Provisions: President can declare national, provincial, or financial emergency under Articles 232, 234, 235.

Conclusion: The 1973 Constitution combines Islamic principles, democratic governance, federalism, and fundamental rights, making it a comprehensive document that continues to guide Pakistan's political system.
UNIT 3

The 18th Amendment

Key features, provincial autonomy, and impact on the federation

Overview of the 18th Amendment (2010)

The 18th Constitutional Amendment was passed on April 8, 2010 by Pakistan's parliament. It was one of the most significant constitutional reforms in Pakistan's history, making 97 changes to the 1973 Constitution.

The 18th Amendment is considered a landmark in Pakistan's democratic history, reversing decades of centralization and military-era amendments.

Key Features

  • Abolished the Concurrent Legislative List (transferring 47 subjects to provinces)
  • Restored the original parliamentary spirit of 1973 Constitution
  • Reversed most changes made by Zia ul-Haq and Musharraf
  • Renamed NWFP to Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (KPK)
  • Strengthened the role of the Prime Minister
  • Limited the President's power to dissolve the National Assembly (removed Article 58-2b)
  • Established independent Election Commission
  • Made constitutional amendments more difficult (required two-thirds majority)

Provincial Autonomy

The most significant achievement of the 18th Amendment was the devolution of power to provinces:

  • Education, health, agriculture, environment — transferred to provinces
  • Planning and Development Division divided between federal and provinces
  • National Finance Commission (NFC) Award revised to give more funds to provinces
  • Provinces gained right to enter into agreements with foreign governments directly
  • FATA (Federally Administered Tribal Areas) reforms initiated

Impact on Federation

Positive Impacts:

  • Provinces better equipped to address local needs and development
  • Reduced sense of alienation in smaller provinces (Sindh, KPK, Balochistan)
  • More democratic and representative governance
  • Strengthened federalism and national unity

Challenges:

  • Some provinces lacked capacity to manage devolved functions
  • Coordination issues between federal and provincial governments
  • Duplication of services in some sectors
  • Education and health service delivery remains uneven

Sample Long Questions & Model Answers

Q1 What are the key features of the 18th Amendment? How did it strengthen provincial autonomy in Pakistan?
Introduction: The 18th Constitutional Amendment (2010), passed during President Asif Ali Zardari's government, is a watershed moment in Pakistan's constitutional history. It fundamentally restructured the relationship between the federal government and the provinces in favor of greater decentralization.

Background: The 1973 Constitution had been repeatedly amended by military rulers — particularly the 8th Amendment (1985) by Zia ul-Haq and the 17th Amendment (2003) by Musharraf — which had concentrated power in the Presidency. The 18th Amendment reversed most of these changes and restored the original parliamentary balance.

Key Features:
1. Abolition of Concurrent List: The 47-item Concurrent Legislative List was abolished, and these subjects were transferred to provinces, including education, health, labor, agriculture, and tourism.

2. Removal of Article 58-2(b): The President lost the power to dissolve the National Assembly, restoring the supremacy of elected parliament.

3. Renaming NWFP: The North-West Frontier Province was renamed Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, fulfilling a long-standing demand of the Pashtun population.

4. Independent Election Commission: Strengthened electoral independence.

Provincial Autonomy: Provinces now control education policy, health infrastructure, local government systems, and environmental regulations. The 7th NFC Award (2009) also increased provincial share of federal divisible pool from 47.5% to 57.5%.

Significance: The amendment addressed the decades-old grievances of smaller provinces, reduced the trust deficit between the federation and provinces, and established a more genuine federal democracy.

Conclusion: The 18th Amendment represents the most progressive constitutional reform in Pakistan's history, transforming Pakistan from a highly centralized state to a genuinely decentralized federation.
UNIT 4

Pakistan–US Relations

Historical background (1947–present), major developments, current status

Historical Background of Pakistan–US Relations

Pakistan–US relations have been described as "transactional" — based on mutual strategic interest rather than shared values. The relationship has gone through several phases of warm alliance and cold estrangement.

Phase 1: Early Alliance (1947–1965)

  • Pakistan joined US-led alliances: SEATO (1954) and CENTO/Baghdad Pact (1955)
  • US provided economic and military aid to Pakistan
  • Pakistan gave US air base access (Peshawar air base used for U-2 spy missions)
  • Relations cooled during 1965 India-Pakistan War (US cut military aid to both)

Phase 2: Nixon Era & Bangladesh Crisis (1969–1971)

  • Pakistan played key role in facilitating Nixon's opening to China (1971)
  • US was silent during Pakistan's crackdown in East Pakistan
  • Relations with India suffered as US tilted toward Pakistan

Phase 3: Soviet-Afghan War (1979–1989)

  • Pakistan became frontline state against Soviet Union in Afghanistan
  • US provided billions in military and economic aid to Pakistan
  • CIA worked with ISI to arm Afghan Mujahideen
  • After Soviet withdrawal, US abandoned the region (Pressler Amendment sanctions)
The Afghan Jihad created long-term consequences: blowback of extremism, drug trade, and regional instability that Pakistan continues to deal with.

Phase 4: Post-9/11 War on Terror (2001–2014)

  • Pakistan joined US "War on Terror" as a "major non-NATO ally"
  • Pakistan received $33 billion+ in Coalition Support Funds
  • Relations strained by drone strikes on Pakistani territory
  • Bin Laden discovered in Abbottabad (2011) — major crisis
  • NATO attack on Pakistani soldiers (Salala incident, 2011)
  • Pakistan closed NATO supply routes for 7 months

Phase 5: Contemporary Relations (2014–Present)

  • US-Pakistan relations declined as US withdrew from Afghanistan (2021)
  • Taliban takeover of Afghanistan — US blamed Pakistan for supporting Taliban
  • Relations under Imran Khan: "Absolutely Not" policy on US bases
  • US-India strategic partnership competes with Pakistan
  • Economic relations: Pakistan is not a major US trading partner
  • FATF grey list affected Pakistani economy and US relations

Sample Long Questions & Model Answers

Q1 Trace the historical evolution of Pakistan–US relations. What are the major factors that have shaped this relationship?
Introduction: Pakistan–US relations since 1947 have been characterized by strategic alliances during periods of mutual need and estrangement when those interests diverged. Analysts often call it a "transactional alliance" rather than a relationship based on shared democratic values.

Cold War Alliance (1950s–1960s): Pakistan, seeking security against India and economic assistance, aligned with the US in the Cold War. Joining SEATO and CENTO brought military and economic aid but also drew Pakistan into Cold War commitments that limited its foreign policy flexibility. The U-2 incident (1960) — where an American spy plane was shot down after departing Pakistan's Peshawar base — demonstrated the risks of this alignment.

Facilitating Sino-American Rapprochement (1971): Pakistan played an irreplaceable role in Henry Kissinger's secret visit to China (1971), facilitated through Pakistani channels. This cemented Nixon's support for Pakistan even during the East Pakistan crisis, though ultimately the US could not prevent Bangladesh's creation.

Afghan Jihad Era (1979–1989): The most transformative period in Pakistan–US relations. Pakistan became the conduit for American support to Afghan Mujahideen fighting Soviet forces. The CIA-ISI cooperation during this period reshaped Pakistan's domestic security establishment and had long-lasting negative consequences in the form of extremism, drug trafficking, and the Afghan civil war that followed.

Post-9/11 Alliance: After 9/11, President Musharraf made a strategic decision to join the US War on Terror, receiving substantial aid but also facing domestic backlash and sovereignty violations through drone strikes. The Abbottabad operation (2011) that killed Osama bin Laden was carried out without Pakistani knowledge, representing a severe breach of trust.

Current Challenges: The US pivot to India as its primary South Asian partner, withdrawal from Afghanistan, and differences over China policy have created structural tensions. Pakistan's growing relationship with China (CPEC) increasingly puts it at odds with US strategic interests.

Conclusion: Pakistan–US relations have historically been determined by strategic calculations rather than shared values or sustained commitment. The challenge for Pakistan is to develop a more balanced foreign policy that serves its long-term national interests rather than being dependent on any single power.
UNIT 5

Current Affairs & Diplomacy

Pakistan's foreign policy, US–Israel–Iran conflict, diplomatic positions and impacts

Pakistan's Foreign Policy — Foundations & Principles

Pakistan's foreign policy is shaped by four core drivers: geographic position (bordering India, Afghanistan, China, Iran), Islamic identity, economic vulnerabilities, and nuclear security imperatives. It operates under a civil-military framework where the armed forces hold significant influence over strategic decisions.

Constitutional Basis: Article 40 — "The State shall endeavor to preserve and strengthen fraternal relations among Muslim countries based on Islamic unity." Article 27 affirms peaceful coexistence and non-interference in the internal affairs of other states.

Core Principles

  • Sovereignty & Non-interference: Pakistan opposes foreign intervention in domestic politics, a principle repeatedly invoked over US drone strikes in FATA (2004–2018) which killed thousands.
  • Islamic Solidarity: Active membership in the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC, founded 1969). Pakistan hosted the OIC Summit in 1974 and recognises Palestine as a core Islamic issue.
  • Nuclear Deterrence: Pakistan conducted nuclear tests on 28 May 1998 (Chagai-I) in response to India's Pokhran-II tests, establishing "minimum credible deterrence" as a cornerstone of national security.
  • Geo-economics over Geo-politics: Coined under PM Imran Khan (2018–2022) and continued under Shehbaz Sharif — prioritising trade corridors (CPEC, SCO) over purely security-driven alliances.
  • Strategic Autonomy: Pakistan avoids permanent military blocs. It left SEATO (1972) and CENTO (1979), reflecting preference for issue-specific alignment.

Key Bilateral Relationships

  • China: "All-weather strategic cooperative partnership." The China–Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), signed April 2015, involves over $62 billion in infrastructure investment — motorways, the Gwadar deep-sea port, and power plants. Gwadar gives China access to the Arabian Sea, reducing its reliance on the Strait of Malacca. (Source: CPEC Authority, Government of Pakistan)
  • India: Hostile relations since Partition (1947). Three full wars (1947, 1965, 1971), Kargil conflict (1999), and repeated cross-border terrorism accusations. The Kashmir dispute remains unresolved at the UN Security Council. Diplomatic ties were downgraded after India revoked Article 370 of its Constitution in August 2019, removing Jammu & Kashmir's special status.
  • USA: Transactional "ally" relationship — see Unit 5 Q&A for full breakdown. Key events include the 2011 Abbottabad raid and the post-2021 Afghanistan withdrawal fallout.
  • Afghanistan: Deeply complex. Pakistan supported the Taliban's first government (1996–2001) and is widely accused of sheltering Taliban leadership during the US occupation. After the Taliban regained power in August 2021, Pakistan initially welcomed the change but relations deteriorated over the Tehreek-e-Taliban Pakistan (TTP), which conducts cross-border attacks from Afghan soil.
  • Saudi Arabia & Gulf: Over 4 million Pakistanis work in the Gulf — remittances exceeded $27 billion in FY2023, accounting for a major share of Pakistan's foreign exchange. Saudi Arabia has provided emergency financial bailouts (e.g., $3 billion deposit in 2018 and again in 2023).
  • Turkey: Growing strategic partnership since 2010s. PM Erdoğan has consistently supported Pakistan's position on Kashmir. Joint defence projects include drone technology transfer (Bayraktar TB2 drones).

Sources: CPEC Authority (cpec.gov.pk); State Bank of Pakistan (sbp.org.pk) for remittance data; Ministry of Foreign Affairs Pakistan (mofa.gov.pk)

The US–Israel–Iran Triangle — Historical & Current Context

Origins of the Conflict

The US–Israel relationship is rooted in post-WWII geopolitics. The US recognised Israel on 14 May 1948, minutes after its declaration of independence. The relationship became a "special alliance" from the 1960s onward, built on shared strategic interests in the Middle East, massive US military aid, and strong domestic lobbying (AIPAC).

The US–Iran relationship collapsed after the Islamic Revolution (1979) and the 444-day hostage crisis at the US Embassy in Tehran. Since then, Iran has been subject to escalating US sanctions and designated a state sponsor of terrorism.

Key Events & Timeline

  • 1979: Iranian Revolution — Ayatollah Khomeini overthrows the US-backed Shah. Iran becomes an Islamic Republic. Hostage crisis (Nov 1979–Jan 1981) permanently ruptures US-Iran ties.
  • 2003: US invasion of Iraq removes Iran's main Sunni rival (Saddam Hussein), inadvertently expanding Iranian influence through Iraq's Shia majority.
  • 2006: Israel–Hezbollah War in Lebanon (34 days). Iran-backed Hezbollah fires thousands of rockets into northern Israel. Israel conducts large-scale military operations but fails to destroy Hezbollah. Seen as Iran's strategic "success."
  • 2015 — JCPOA (Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action): Historic nuclear deal signed between Iran and P5+1 (US, UK, France, Russia, China + Germany). Iran agreed to limit uranium enrichment in exchange for sanctions relief. Strongly opposed by Israel (Netanyahu addressed US Congress to oppose it in March 2015).
  • 2018: President Trump withdraws the US from the JCPOA ("maximum pressure" policy), reimposing harsh sanctions. Iran gradually resumes uranium enrichment beyond agreed limits.
  • Jan 2020: US drone strike kills Iran's top general, Qasem Soleimani, at Baghdad airport. Iran retaliates with ballistic missile strikes on US bases in Iraq (Al-Asad airbase). Massive regional tension. Pakistan calls for restraint.
  • Oct 7, 2023 — Hamas Attack on Israel: Hamas launches a coordinated large-scale attack from Gaza, killing approximately 1,200 Israelis and taking over 250 hostages. The deadliest attack on Jews since the Holocaust.
  • Oct 2023–present — Gaza War: Israel launches an intensive military campaign in Gaza. By mid-2024, over 37,000 Palestinians killed (UN OCHA data), 1.5 million displaced, and much of northern Gaza reduced to rubble. The UN General Assembly passed multiple resolutions calling for a ceasefire (which the US vetoed at the Security Council level, three times by May 2024).
  • Apr 2024: Iran directly attacks Israel for the first time in history — over 300 drones and missiles fired in response to an Israeli airstrike on Iran's consulate in Damascus (April 1, 2024). Israel intercepts most projectiles with US/UK/Jordanian assistance. Israel retaliates with a limited strike on Isfahan, Iran.
  • 2024 Lebanon Escalation: Israel expands military operations into Lebanon, assassinating Hezbollah leader Hassan Nasrallah in Beirut (September 2024) and conducting a large ground invasion. Iran-backed axis suffers significant setbacks.

Sources: UN OCHA (ochaopt.org); BBC News; Al Jazeera English; IAEA reports on Iran's nuclear programme; US State Department

Iran's "Axis of Resistance"

Iran has built a network of non-state proxies across the region to project power and deter direct attack:

  • Hezbollah (Lebanon): Iran's most powerful proxy. Receives ~$700 million/year from Iran (US estimates). Has 130,000+ rockets. Fought Israel in 2006 and sustained Israeli strikes since Oct 2023.
  • Hamas (Gaza): Iran provides funding and weapons, though Hamas is Sunni — a rare sectarian exception in Iran's Shia-led network. Hamas political bureau is based in Qatar.
  • Houthis (Yemen): Iran-aligned movement controlling northern Yemen including Sanaa. Since Nov 2023, launched over 100 drone/missile attacks on Red Sea shipping, disrupting global trade (Suez Canal traffic fell ~42% by early 2024). US and UK launched retaliatory strikes on Houthi targets in Yemen from January 2024.
  • Shia militias in Iraq & Syria: Groups like Kataib Hezbollah attacked US bases repeatedly; killed 3 US soldiers at Jordan's Tower 22 base in January 2024.

Israel's Strategic Concerns

  • Iran enriching uranium to 60% purity (weapons-grade is ~90%). IAEA reports Iran has enough enriched uranium for multiple nuclear devices if further processed.
  • Netanyahu's government has repeatedly threatened military strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities.
  • Israel conducted covert operations inside Iran — assassination of nuclear scientists (2010–2020), Stuxnet cyberattack on Natanz facility (2010, attributed to US-Israel), and an explosion at the Fordow enrichment facility in 2020.

Sources: IAEA Director-General Reports (iaea.org); Council on Foreign Relations (cfr.org); Institute for Science and International Security (isis-online.org)

Pakistan's Position on the US–Israel–Iran Conflict

Pakistan's response is shaped by a complex web of constraints: Islamic solidarity, Saudi/Gulf economic dependence, Iran as a neighbour, fear of US sanctions, and domestic public opinion that is overwhelmingly pro-Palestinian.

1. Palestine & Israel

Pakistan does not recognise Israel and has maintained this position since 1948. Pakistani passports until 2015 explicitly stated "valid for all countries except Israel." Pakistan supported every UN General Assembly resolution calling for Palestinian statehood.

  • OIC Platform: Pakistan has used the OIC to advocate for Palestinians. It hosted the OIC Extraordinary Summit on Palestine in Dec 2017 after the US recognised Jerusalem as Israel's capital (Trump's declaration).
  • Gaza War 2023: PM Shehbaz Sharif condemned the Israeli offensive at the UN General Assembly (Sept 2024), calling for an immediate ceasefire and humanitarian corridors. Pakistan voted for the UNGA ceasefire resolution (Dec 2023).
  • No normalisation: Despite US pressure and the Abraham Accords (2020 — UAE, Bahrain, Sudan, Morocco normalised relations with Israel), Pakistan's civilian and military leadership unanimously rejected normalisation. Army Chief Gen. Asim Munir stated in 2023 that Pakistan would only recognise Israel upon establishment of an independent Palestinian state.
Pakistan's consistent position: A two-state solution based on pre-1967 borders, with East Jerusalem as the capital of Palestine and the right of return for Palestinian refugees — in line with UN Resolution 194 (1948) and UN Resolution 242 (1967).

2. Iran Relations

Pakistan shares an 800+ km border with Iran. Relations are complicated by sectarianism (Pakistan's Sunni majority vs Iran's Shia leadership), but both states maintain formal diplomatic ties.

  • Iran–Pakistan Gas Pipeline (IP Pipeline / "Peace Pipeline"): Proposed since 1990s, partially constructed on Iran's side. Pakistan has been unable to complete its section due to US sanctions threats (CAATSA). As of 2024, the pipeline remains incomplete — Pakistan faces a $18 billion penalty from Iran for breach of contract.
  • Jan 2024 — Cross-border strikes: In an extraordinary escalation, Iran launched missile strikes on Pakistani territory in Balochistan (Jan 16, 2024), claiming to target the Jaish al-Adl militant group. Pakistan recalled its ambassador, expelled Iran's ambassador, and conducted retaliatory strikes inside Iran two days later — marking a historic low in bilateral relations. Relations were restored within weeks due to mutual economic interests.
  • Balochistan dimension: Iran accuses Pakistan of allowing Sunni extremist groups like Jaish al-Adl to operate from Pakistani territory; Pakistan makes mirror accusations about Iranian support for Baloch separatists.

Sources: Dawn newspaper (dawn.com); The Express Tribune; Reuters reporting on Jan 2024 cross-border strikes

3. Saudi Arabia — The Economic Anchor

  • Over 2.6 million Pakistanis work in Saudi Arabia — the largest single source of remittances.
  • 2015 Yemen Crisis: Saudi Arabia requested Pakistani military participation in the Saudi-led coalition against Houthi forces. Pakistan's parliament voted on April 10, 2015 to reject military involvement — a landmark assertion of parliamentary oversight over foreign policy. Pakistan offered only logistical and diplomatic support.
  • Saudi Arabia has provided emergency financial support multiple times: $1.5 billion grant (2018), $3 billion deposit (2018, repeated in 2021 and 2023 during Pakistan's IMF bailout crises).
  • Crown Prince MBS's Vision 2030 has invited Pakistani skilled workers. Pakistan-Saudi trade reached $6.6 billion in 2022–23.

4. The US Factor

  • Pakistan is wary of US secondary sanctions (CAATSA) that could penalise Pakistan for deals with Iran or Russia.
  • Despite diplomatic tensions, Pakistan cooperates with the US on counter-terrorism (Financial Action Task Force — Pakistan was on the FATF "Grey List" 2018–2022, exiting after meeting compliance conditions).

Q3 Analyse the January 2024 Pakistan–Iran cross-border strikes. What does this episode reveal about Pakistan's foreign policy challenges?
Introduction: The January 2024 Pakistan–Iran cross-border exchange was one of the most dramatic foreign policy incidents in Pakistan's recent history — revealing how regional conflicts and domestic security failures interact to create international crises.

Sequence of Events:
• Jan 16, 2024: Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched ballistic missile and drone strikes on Pakistani territory in Balochistan's Panjgur district, killing 2 children and injuring 3 others. Iran claimed to target bases of Jaish al-Adl, a Sunni extremist group that had killed 13 Iranian police officers in a Dec 2023 attack inside Iran.
• Jan 17, 2024: Pakistan immediately recalled its ambassador from Tehran, expelled Iran's ambassador, and summoned Iran's chargé d'affaires. PM Anwaar ul Haq Kakar issued a strong condemnation, calling it a "blatant violation of Pakistani sovereignty."
• Jan 18, 2024: Pakistan conducted retaliatory military strikes — using drones and rockets — on Balochistan Province of Iran (Saravan district), killing 9 people including members of Baloch separatist groups. Pakistan's ISPR statement called it a "precision military operation."
• Within two weeks, both countries restored ambassadors and held foreign minister meetings, prioritising bilateral economic interests over the crisis.

What This Reveals:
1. Governance gaps in Balochistan: The presence of Jaish al-Adl on Pakistani soil — operating across the Iran border — indicates failure of writ of state in tribal border regions. Iran's frustration had been building for years with Pakistan's inaction.
2. Iran's assertiveness: Iran's willingness to strike nuclear-armed Pakistan reflects its post-Gaza War confidence and its model of "forward deterrence" through direct military action against perceived threats.
3. Limits of Pakistan–Iran relations: Despite shared opposition to Israel and US hegemony, sectarian and security tensions mean Pakistan–Iran relations are inherently fragile.
4. Rapid restoration: The quick normalisation (within 2 weeks) shows economic pragmatism overrides strategic posturing — Pakistan needs Iranian gas (IP pipeline), and Iran needs Pakistani support at the OIC.

Conclusion: The Jan 2024 episode exemplifies how Pakistan's domestic security failures create foreign policy crises. It also demonstrates Pakistan's capacity for calibrated retaliation (reasserting deterrence) without escalation — a reflection of the mature strategic culture a nuclear state must maintain.
