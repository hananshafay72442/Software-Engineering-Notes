Ch. 2.5 — Derivatives of Trigonometric Functions · All Formulas
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\cos x] = -\sin x$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\tan x] = \sec^2 x$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\cot x] = -\csc^2 x$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\sec x] = \sec x\tan x$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\csc x] = -\csc x\cot x$
Key limits used in proofs:
$\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sin h}{h} = 1$
$\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1-\cos h}{h} = 0$
Derived from: $\tan x = \dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x}$, $\sec x = \dfrac{1}{\cos x}$, etc. — quotient rule applied to these identities gives formulas (5)–(8).
Why it matters: Oscillating systems — spring-mass, pendulums, AC circuits, ocean waves — all use $\sin$ and $\cos$. Their derivatives tell us velocity and acceleration of the oscillation instantly.
Method — differentiating trig functions: Use the formulas directly, combine with Product/Quotient Rule for products like $x\sin x$ or $\frac{\sin x}{1+\cos x}$. Always simplify using trig identities ($\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$) when possible.
Ch. 2.6 — The Chain Rule · All Formulas
Leibniz form (two-variable):
If $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$, then
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy}{du}\cdot\dfrac{du}{dx}$
Function form:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$
Generalized derivative formulas (u = g(x)):
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[u^r] = ru^{r-1}\dfrac{du}{dx}$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\sin u] = \cos u\dfrac{du}{dx}$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\cos u] = -\sin u\dfrac{du}{dx}$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\tan u] = \sec^2 u\dfrac{du}{dx}$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\sec u] = \sec u\tan u\dfrac{du}{dx}$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln u] = \dfrac{1}{u}\dfrac{du}{dx}$  (see Ch. 6.2)
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^u] = e^u\dfrac{du}{dx}$  (see Ch. 6.3)
Why it matters: Almost every real formula is a composition — e.g. the volume of a balloon depends on radius, which depends on time. The chain rule connects those layers of dependence.
Method — applying the chain rule:
  • 1Identify the outside function $f$ and the inside function $g(x)$.
  • 2Differentiate the outside: $f'(g(x))$ — evaluate $f'$ at the inside, leaving $g(x)$ untouched.
  • 3Multiply by the derivative of the inside: $g'(x)$.
  • 4For nested compositions (chain within chain), repeat steps 1–3 from the outside in.
Ch. 6.2 — Derivatives of Logarithmic Functions · All Formulas
Logarithm Laws (all bases):
$\ln(a \cdot b) = \ln a + \ln b$
$\ln\!\left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right) = \ln a - \ln b$
$\ln(a^r) = r\ln a$
$\ln 1 = 0 \quad;\quad \ln e = 1$
$e^{\ln x} = x \quad;\quad \ln(e^x) = x$

Change of Base:
$\log_b a = \dfrac{\ln a}{\ln b}$
Derivative Formulas:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \dfrac{1}{x}$

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln|x|] = \dfrac{1}{x}$

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln u] = \dfrac{u'}{u}$  (chain rule form)

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\log_b x] = \dfrac{1}{x\ln b}$

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[\log_b u] = \dfrac{u'}{u\ln b}$  (chain rule form)
Example 1 — ln with chain rule:
$y = \ln(x^2+1)$,   $u=x^2+1$, $u'=2x$

$y' = \dfrac{2x}{x^2+1}$
Example 2 — log base 10:
$y = \log_{10}(3x)$,   $u=3x$, $u'=3$

$y' = \dfrac{3}{3x\ln 10} = \dfrac{1}{x\ln 10}$
Example 3 — simplify first:
$y = \ln(x^2\sqrt{x+1})$
$= 2\ln x + \tfrac{1}{2}\ln(x+1)$

$y' = \dfrac{2}{x} + \dfrac{1}{2(x+1)}$
Why it matters: Logarithms appear everywhere — pH (chemistry), decibels (acoustics), the Richter scale, information theory, compound growth. The formula $\tfrac{d}{dx}[\ln x]=\tfrac{1}{x}$ fills the gap in the power rule (which fails at $r=-1$) and gives us $\int\tfrac{1}{x}dx = \ln|x|+C$.
Logarithmic differentiation: For messy products/quotients/variable powers, take $\ln$ of both sides, use log laws to break it apart, then differentiate implicitly. Great for $y=x^x$ or $y=\dfrac{x^3\sqrt{x+1}}{(2x-1)^4}$.
Ch. 6.3 — Derivatives of Exponential Functions · All Formulas
Core Derivative Formulas:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x$

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^u] = e^u\dfrac{du}{dx}$  (chain rule form)

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[a^x] = a^x\ln a$

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[a^u] = a^u\ln a\cdot\dfrac{du}{dx}$  (chain rule form)

Special case: $a=e$ gives $e^x\ln e = e^x\cdot 1 = e^x$ ✓
Why $\dfrac{d}{dx}[a^x] = a^x \ln a$:
Rewrite $a^x = e^{x\ln a}$ (since $a = e^{\ln a}$)

Then by chain rule:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{x\ln a}] = e^{x\ln a}\cdot\ln a = a^x\ln a$

The $\ln a$ factor is the "cost" of using a base other than $e$. When $a=e$, $\ln e=1$ and it disappears.
Exponential Properties (review):
$a^0 = 1 \quad;\quad a^1 = a$
$a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$
$\dfrac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}$
$(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$
$(ab)^n = a^n b^n$
Example 1 — basic:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{3x}]$

$u=3x$, $u'=3$
$= e^{3x}\cdot 3 = 3e^{3x}$
Example 2 — base $a$:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[2^x]$

$a=2$, so multiply by $\ln 2$
$= 2^x\ln 2$
Example 3 — chain rule + $a^u$:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[5^{x^2}]$

$u=x^2$, $u'=2x$, $a=5$
$= 5^{x^2}\cdot\ln 5\cdot 2x = 2x\ln 5\cdot 5^{x^2}$
Example 4 — $e^u$ with product:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2 e^x]$   (product rule)

$= 2x\cdot e^x + x^2\cdot e^x$
$= xe^x(2+x)$
Why it matters: Exponential functions model virtually every continuous growth or decay process — population growth, radioactive decay, compound interest, charging capacitors, cooling/heating (Newton's Law of Cooling). The fact that $\tfrac{d}{dx}[e^x]=e^x$ makes $e$ the "natural" base: the function that is its own rate of change, which is exactly what exponential growth means.
Key contrast with power rule: The power rule $\tfrac{d}{dx}[x^n]=nx^{n-1}$ handles variable base, fixed exponent. The exponential rule $\tfrac{d}{dx}[a^x]=a^x\ln a$ handles fixed base, variable exponent. These are fundamentally different — do not mix them up!

Ch. 3.2 — Relative Extrema & Graphing Polynomials · All Formulas & Theorems
Critical Point: $x_0$ is a critical point of $f$ if $f'(x_0)=0$ (called a stationary point) or $f'(x_0)$ does not exist. Extrema can only happen at critical points — but not every critical point is an extremum.
🔺 Relative Minima & Maxima
A relative (local) maximum is a point where $f$ is higher than all nearby points — a local "peak." A relative (local) minimum is a point where $f$ is lower than all nearby points — a local "valley." There are two tests to classify them:
📈 Test 1 — First Derivative Test
Core Idea: $f'(x)$ tells you whether the function is increasing (going uphill) or decreasing (going downhill) at any point. At a critical point, look at what happens to $f'$ just before and just after it — the direction change reveals whether you're at a peak or a valley.
Case 1 → Relative Maximum

$f'$ goes from positive (+) to negative (−)

Left of $x_0$: $f' > 0$ → function rising ↗
Right of $x_0$: $f' < 0$ → function falling ↘

Rising then falling = a PEAK = Relative Maximum
Case 2 → Relative Minimum

$f'$ goes from negative (−) to positive (+)

Left of $x_0$: $f' < 0$ → function falling ↘
Right of $x_0$: $f' > 0$ → function rising ↗

Falling then rising = a VALLEY = Relative Minimum
Case 3 → Neither: If $f'$ has the same sign on both sides (both + or both −), the function doesn't reverse direction — no peak or valley, just a flat inflection point.
📋 How to Apply it — Step by Step:
  • 1Find $f'(x)$ and solve $f'(x) = 0$. Also find any $x$ where $f'$ doesn't exist. These are your critical points.
  • 2Draw a number line and mark all critical points on it. They split the line into intervals.
  • 3Pick a test value inside each interval and plug it into $f'(x)$. Record the sign (+ or −) for that whole interval.
  • 4Read the sign changes at each critical point: $(+\to-)$ = max, $(-\to+)$ = min, same sign = neither.
  • 5Find the $y$-value at each extremum by plugging $x_0$ back into the original $f(x)$.
Fully Worked Example:  $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$

Step 1: $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 3(x^2-1) = 3(x-1)(x+1)$
    Set $f'=0$: $x = -1$ and $x = 1$  ← critical points

Step 2 & 3: Sign chart for $f'(x)$:

     ←——————|——————|——————→
           x=−1   x=+1
test: x=−2    x=0    x=2
f'(−2)=9   f'(0)=−3  f'(2)=9
   (+)      (−)      (+)

Step 4:
    At $x=-1$: sign changes $(+) \to (-)$  ⟹  Relative Maximum
    At $x=+1$: sign changes $(-) \to (+)$  ⟹  Relative Minimum

Step 5:
    $f(-1) = (-1)^3 - 3(-1) = -1+3 = 2$  ⟹  Relative Max at $(-1,\;2)$
    $f(1) = (1)^3 - 3(1) = 1-3 = -2$  ⟹  Relative Min at $(1,\;-2)$
✅ Advantage: Works even when $f'(x_0)$ doesn't exist (cusps, sharp corners). This is the most universal and reliable test.
📐 Test 2 — Second Derivative Test
Core Idea: $f''(x)$ measures concavity — whether the curve is bending upward (like a bowl ∪) or bending downward (like a hill ∩). At a stationary point where the function is momentarily flat ($f'=0$), the concavity tells you everything: if it bends upward, you must be at the bottom of a bowl = minimum; if it bends downward, you must be at the top of a hill = maximum.
$f''(x_0) > 0$ → Relative Minimum

Curve is concave up ∪ at $x_0$
(bowl shape — opens upward)

You are at the bottom of the bowl
⟹ Relative Minimum

Think: smiley face ☺ — the middle is the lowest point.
$f''(x_0) < 0$ → Relative Maximum

Curve is concave down ∩ at $x_0$
(hill shape — opens downward)

You are at the top of the hill
⟹ Relative Maximum

Think: sad face ☹ — the middle is the highest point.
$f''(x_0) = 0$ → Inconclusive ⚠️
The second derivative test gives no information. You must fall back to the First Derivative Test (sign chart method) to classify the critical point.
📋 How to Apply it — Step by Step:
  • 1Find $f'(x)$ and solve $f'(x) = 0$ to get the stationary points. (Points where $f'$ doesn't exist cannot be tested this way.)
  • 2Find $f''(x)$ by differentiating $f'(x)$ once more.
  • 3Plug each stationary point $x_0$ into $f''(x)$. Evaluate the sign of $f''(x_0)$.
  • 4Read the result: $f''(x_0)>0$ → min, $f''(x_0)<0$ → max, $f''(x_0)=0$ → go back to Test 1.
  • 5Find the $y$-value by computing $f(x_0)$ to state the full coordinate of the extremum.
Fully Worked Example:  $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$  (same function as above)

Step 1: $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 0$  ⟹  $x^2 = 1$  ⟹  $x = -1$ and $x = 1$

Step 2: $f''(x) = \dfrac{d}{dx}[3x^2 - 3] = 6x$

Step 3 & 4:
    At $x = -1$:  $f''(-1) = 6(-1) = -6$  < 0  ⟹  Concave Down → Relative Maximum
    At $x = +1$:  $f''(1) = 6(1) = +6$  > 0  ⟹  Concave Up → Relative Minimum

Step 5:
    $f(-1) = -1+3 = 2$  ⟹  Relative Max at $(-1,\;2)$ ✓
    $f(1) = 1-3 = -2$  ⟹  Relative Min at $(1,\;-2)$ ✓

Same answer as the First Derivative Test — both tests always agree when both are applicable.
⚠️ Limitation: Only works at stationary points (where $f'(x_0)=0$). If the critical point comes from $f'$ being undefined, you must use the First Derivative Test instead.
⚖️ WHICH TEST SHOULD YOU USE?
Use First Derivative Test when:
• $f'(x_0)$ doesn't exist at the critical point
• $f''$ is hard to compute
• Second Derivative Test gives $f''=0$
• You want 100% certainty (always works)
Use Second Derivative Test when:
• Critical point comes from $f'(x_0)=0$
• $f''$ is easy to compute
• You want a quick one-step answer
• You don't need to build a sign chart
Why it matters: Relative maxima and minima tell you local peaks and valleys — e.g. a profit function's local max shows when a pricing strategy is most effective in a range, even if it's not the all-time best.
Ch. 3.2 — Absolute Minima & Maxima · Extreme Value Theorem
An absolute (global) maximum is the single highest value $f$ attains over its entire domain (or a closed interval). An absolute (global) minimum is the single lowest value. Unlike relative extrema — which are just "locally" highest or lowest — absolute extrema are the overall winner and loser.
Extreme Value Theorem (EVT):
If $f$ is continuous on a closed interval $[a,b]$, then $f$ is guaranteed to attain both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum on $[a,b]$.

Note: Both conditions matter — continuity AND a closed interval. If either fails, the guarantee is gone.
Where do absolute extrema live? On a closed interval $[a,b]$, the absolute max/min must be at one of:
  • ①A critical point inside $(a,b)$ (where $f'=0$ or $f'$ doesn't exist)
  • ②An endpoint: $x=a$ or $x=b$
This is called the Closed Interval Method. You simply evaluate $f$ at all critical points + endpoints, then compare.
📋 Step-by-Step: Finding Absolute Extrema on $[a,b]$
  • 1Compute $f'(x)$ and find all critical points in the open interval $(a,b)$ — solve $f'(x)=0$ and find where $f'$ is undefined.
  • 2Evaluate $f$ at every critical point found in step 1.
  • 3Evaluate $f$ at the two endpoints: $f(a)$ and $f(b)$.
  • 4Compare all the values from steps 2–3. The largest is the absolute maximum; the smallest is the absolute minimum.
Example: Find abs. extrema of $f(x)=x^3-3x$ on $[-2,\,3]$.

Step 1: $f'(x)=3x^2-3=0 \Rightarrow x=\pm 1$. Both in $(-2,3)$ ✓
Step 2: $f(-1)=2$, $\;f(1)=-2$
Step 3: $f(-2)=(-2)^3-3(-2)=-8+6=-2$, $\;f(3)=27-9=18$
Step 4: Values: $\{2,\,-2,\,-2,\,18\}$
⟹ Absolute Max = 18 at $x=3$ (endpoint!)
⟹ Absolute Min = −2 at $x=1$ and $x=-2$
Relative vs Absolute — Key Difference:
A relative max is just the highest point nearby — like a small hill. An absolute max is the highest point everywhere on the domain — like the tallest mountain. Every absolute extremum is also a relative extremum (unless it's an endpoint), but a relative extremum is NOT necessarily absolute.
Why it matters: Real optimisation always asks for the absolute best — max profit over a quarter, min cost over a production run, highest/lowest temperature over a day. The closed interval method gives you a reliable, mechanical algorithm that never misses the answer.
Ch. 4.1 — The Area Problem & Why We Need Integration
The Big Question: Differentiation tells us the rate at which things change (velocity, slope, growth rate). But what if we know the rate and want the total? That's where integration comes in — it is the mathematical tool for accumulation.
🤔 The Problem Differentiation Can't Solve
Suppose a car travels at a changing speed — say $v(t) = t^2$ m/s. How far did it travel from $t=0$ to $t=3$? You can't just multiply speed × time, because the speed isn't constant. You need to add up infinitely many tiny distances over infinitely small time intervals. That process is integration.
The geometric version: Find the area under the curve $y = f(x)$ between $x=a$ and $x=b$.

We can't use simple geometry (the shape isn't a rectangle or triangle) — so we approximate with many thin rectangles, then take the limit:

$A = \displaystyle\lim_{n\to+\infty}\sum_{k=1}^{n} f(x_k^*)\,\Delta x$

This limit, when it exists, is the definite integral $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.
💡 Why Integration Solves So Much
Integration isn't just about area — it's the universal tool for any problem involving total accumulated change:
  • →Physics: Know velocity? Integrate to get position. Know acceleration? Integrate twice to get position.
  • →Engineering: Know force as a function of distance? Integrate to get total work done.
  • →Economics: Know a marginal cost function? Integrate to get total cost over a production range.
  • →Biology: Know a population growth rate? Integrate to find the total population over time.
  • →Electricity: Know electric current (charge per second)? Integrate to get total charge delivered.
The Fundamental Bridge (Newton–Leibniz):
If $A(x)$ = area accumulated from $a$ to $x$, then:
$A'(x) = f(x)$

Meaning: area and antiderivative are the same thing. To find exact area, just find an antiderivative! Integration suddenly becomes powerful — instead of computing millions of rectangles, you find $F(b)-F(a)$.
The core insight: Differentiation and integration are opposites of each other (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus). Differentiation breaks things into rates; integration rebuilds the total from the rates. Together, they let us model essentially any continuous process in science and engineering — which is why calculus is the language of the universe.
Ch. 4.2 — The Indefinite Integral · All Formulas
Antiderivative: $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$ on an interval if $F'(x)=f(x)$ for all $x$ there.
General antiderivative (Theorem 4.2.2): If $F(x)$ is any antiderivative of $f$, then all antiderivatives have the form $F(x)+C$ for some constant $C$.
Notation: $\displaystyle\int f(x)\,dx = F(x) + C$

$\displaystyle\int x^r\,dx = \frac{x^{r+1}}{r+1}+C \quad (r\ne -1)$
$\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C$
$\displaystyle\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C$
$\displaystyle\int b^x\,dx = \frac{b^x}{\ln b} + C$
$\displaystyle\int \cos x\,dx = \sin x + C$
$\displaystyle\int \sin x\,dx = -\cos x + C$
$\displaystyle\int \sec^2 x\,dx = \tan x + C$
$\displaystyle\int \csc^2 x\,dx = -\cot x + C$
$\displaystyle\int \sec x\tan x\,dx = \sec x + C$
$\displaystyle\int \csc x\cot x\,dx = -\csc x + C$
Properties (Theorem 4.2.3):
$\displaystyle\int cf(x)\,dx = c\int f(x)\,dx$

$\displaystyle\int [f+g]\,dx = \int f\,dx + \int g\,dx$

$\displaystyle\int [f-g]\,dx = \int f\,dx - \int g\,dx$
Initial-Value Problem: Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x),\; y(x_0)=y_0$. Integrate to get $y=F(x)+C$, then substitute $(x_0,y_0)$ to find $C$.
Why it matters: Integration "undoes" differentiation — given a rate of change (velocity, growth rate, power), integration recovers the total (position, population, energy). It's essential in physics, biology, and engineering.
Method — evaluating indefinite integrals:
  • 1Break the integrand using sum/difference and constant-multiple rules.
  • 2Apply the power rule: $\int x^r\,dx = \frac{x^{r+1}}{r+1}+C$ (rewrite roots and negative powers first).
  • 3Apply trig formulas directly for $\sin,\cos,\sec^2$, etc.
  • 4Always add $+C$. Check by differentiating your answer.
Ch. 4.3 — Integration by Substitution (u-Sub) · Easy Step-by-Step Guide
What is u-Substitution? Think of it as the reverse of the Chain Rule. When you differentiated $f(g(x))$ you got $f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$. U-substitution undoes exactly that: you spot that pattern in an integral and simplify it by introducing a single variable $u$ to replace the "inside" part. The goal is always to turn a complicated integral into a simple one.
The Core Formula (you must memorise this):

If $u = g(x)$, then $du = g'(x)\,dx$, and:

$\displaystyle\int f\!\left(g(x)\right)\cdot g'(x)\,dx \;=\; \int f(u)\,du \;=\; F(u)+C \;=\; F(g(x))+C$

In plain English: swap the inside function for $u$, swap $g'(x)\,dx$ for $du$, integrate simply, then swap back.
🪜 The 6 Steps (follow these every single time)
  • 1Spot the "inside" function. Look for something being raised to a power, or inside a trig function, or under a root. That's your $u$. Set $u = g(x)$.
  • 2Differentiate $u$ to get $du$. Compute $\dfrac{du}{dx} = g'(x)$, then write $du = g'(x)\,dx$. This tells you what to replace $g'(x)\,dx$ with.
  • 3Check that $g'(x)$ is in the integrand. If it's there (maybe with a different constant in front), great! If it's missing entirely, u-sub probably won't work here — try a different $u$.
  • 4Substitute everything. Replace the inside function with $u$ and replace $g'(x)\,dx$ with $du$. The integral should now be entirely in terms of $u$ — no $x$'s left.
  • 5Integrate in $u$. This should now be a simple integral — use the power rule, trig formulas, etc. Get $F(u) + C$.
  • 6Back-substitute. Replace $u$ with $g(x)$ to write your final answer in terms of $x$. Always add $+C$. Optionally, check by differentiating.
📝 Three Worked Examples (Easy → Medium → Tricky)
Example 1 (Easy) — Perfect match:
$\displaystyle\int (x^2+1)^{50}\cdot 2x\,dx$

Step 1: Inside = $x^2+1$. Set $u = x^2+1$.
Step 2: $du = 2x\,dx$
Step 3: $2x\,dx$ is right there ✓
Step 4: $\displaystyle\int u^{50}\,du$
Step 5: $= \dfrac{u^{51}}{51} + C$
Step 6: $= \dfrac{(x^2+1)^{51}}{51} + C$ ✅
Example 2 (Medium) — Adjusting a constant:
$\displaystyle\int x^2(x^3+5)^8\,dx$

Step 1: Inside = $x^3+5$. Set $u = x^3+5$.
Step 2: $du = 3x^2\,dx \;\Rightarrow\; x^2\,dx = \dfrac{du}{3}$
Step 3: $x^2\,dx$ is there, just off by factor of 3 ✓
Step 4: $\displaystyle\int u^8\cdot\frac{du}{3} = \frac{1}{3}\int u^8\,du$
Step 5: $= \dfrac{1}{3}\cdot\dfrac{u^9}{9}+C = \dfrac{u^9}{27}+C$
Step 6: $= \dfrac{(x^3+5)^9}{27}+C$ ✅
Example 3 (Trig) — u-sub with trig functions:
$\displaystyle\int \cos(5x)\,dx$

Step 1: Inside of $\cos(\,)$ is $5x$. Set $u = 5x$.
Step 2: $du = 5\,dx \;\Rightarrow\; dx = \dfrac{du}{5}$
Step 3: $dx$ is in the integrand (as the $dx$ itself) ✓
Step 4: $\displaystyle\int \cos(u)\cdot\frac{du}{5} = \frac{1}{5}\int\cos(u)\,du$
Step 5: $= \dfrac{1}{5}\sin(u)+C$
Step 6: $= \dfrac{1}{5}\sin(5x)+C$ ✅

✔ Check: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[\dfrac{1}{5}\sin(5x)\right] = \dfrac{1}{5}\cdot\cos(5x)\cdot 5 = \cos(5x)$ ✓
⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid
❌ Forgetting to change $dx$:
Don't just replace $u$ and leave $dx$. You must always express $dx$ in terms of $du$ and fully substitute.
❌ Leaving $x$ in the final answer:
After integrating in $u$, you must back-substitute $u = g(x)$. The final answer must be in $x$, not $u$.
❌ Forgetting the constant multiplier:
If $du = 3x^2\,dx$ but you only have $x^2\,dx$, you must bring out the $\tfrac{1}{3}$ in front of the integral.
❌ Choosing the wrong $u$:
A good $u$ is the "inside" function whose derivative also appears in the integrand. If after substituting you still have $x$'s left, you chose the wrong $u$.
Why it matters: The basic integration formulas only handle simple functions like $x^r$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$. Real-world integrals almost always involve compositions — $\sin(3x^2)$, $(t^2+1)^{10}$, etc. U-substitution is the key that unlocks all of them. Master these 6 steps and you can handle the vast majority of integrals you'll encounter.
