Narrative Style: Elements of Story
Narrative Writing — Quick Overview

Narrative writing tells a story with a beginning, middle, and end. Something must happen — there must be forward momentum, characters, and stakes.

The 6 Core Elements
ElementWhat It MeansExample / Tip
PlotSequence of events: Exposition → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action → ResolutionA boy sneaks out at night. He gets caught. He learns something.
CharacterShow who they are through action, not description"He left without saying goodbye." — more powerful than "He was cold."
SettingWhere + when — it creates atmosphere, not just backdrop"The alley smelled of rain and old newspapers."
ConflictThe problem that drives the story (vs self / others / nature / society)A student torn between lying to protect a friend or telling the truth.
ThemeThe underlying idea — don't state it, show it through eventsA story about a lost key can really be about trust.
Voice1st person (I) = intimate; 3rd person = more distance and control"I ran." vs "She ran." — different emotional effect.
Key Techniques for Examinations
✦ Examiner Checklist
  • Open with a strong hook — action, dialogue, or a striking image.
  • Vary sentence length: short sentences create tension; longer ones build atmosphere.
  • Use at least one piece of dialogue to bring the scene to life.
  • Ground the story in a specific, concrete setting.
  • End purposefully — a revelation, a quiet image, or an open question.
Strong Opening Example"The day I stole the money, the sky was the colour of old tin." — Creates immediate intrigue, establishes atmosphere, and raises questions.

Descriptive Style
Descriptive Writing — Quick Overview

Descriptive writing immerses the reader in a scene using precise language and all five senses. Unlike narrative, there is no plot — the goal is to make the reader feel they are there.

SenseWeak VersionStrong Version
SightThe market was busy.Stalls jostled for space beneath striped canvas awnings, each spilling its colours onto the wet cobblestones.
SoundIt was noisy.A radio crackled from somewhere inside, its tinny music swallowed by the roar of the crowd.
SmellIt smelled nice.The air was thick with cardamom, frying onions, and the faint sweetness of over-ripe fruit.
TouchThe surface was rough.She ran her fingers along the wall; the plaster crumbled at her touch like stale bread.
TasteThe food tasted good.The tea left a bitter, smoky residue on the back of her tongue.
Key Techniques
Simile

A comparison using like or as.

Example"The fog moved through the streets like a slow tide, swallowing lampposts whole."
Metaphor

A direct comparison — stating one thing is another.

Example"The city was a machine that never stopped — grinding, turning, consuming."
Personification

Giving human qualities to non-human things.

Example"The wind whispered through the eaves and tugged at the loose shutter, demanding to be let in."
Pathetic Fallacy

Using weather or environment to reflect a character's mood or the emotional tone of a scene.

Example"The morning of the funeral arrived grey and still, as though the sky itself had nothing left to say."
Show, Don't Tell

The cardinal rule of descriptive writing. Instead of naming an emotion or state, show it through specific, concrete detail.

  • Telling "He was frightened."
  • Showing "His hand reached for the door handle but would not close around it."
Structure & Rhythm
  • Vary sentence length deliberately. Short sentences create impact and pace. Longer, flowing sentences slow the reader down and build atmosphere.
  • Use a zoom technique: open wide (the whole scene), then zoom into one specific, telling detail.
  • End on a resonant image or detail — something that lingers.
✦ Golden Rule

Choose precise, unexpected words over vague, overused ones. "Crimson" is stronger than "red." "Muttered" is stronger than "said." "Shattered" is stronger than "broke."

Personality & Lifestyle Idioms

These idioms describe personality types and daily habits — many using animals or objects. Learn them in opposite pairs where possible.

🌙 Night Owl ↔ 🌅 Early Bird
IdiomMeaningOpposite
*Night owlA person who stays up late and is most active at nightEarly bird
*Early birdA person who wakes up early; most alert and productive in the morningNight owl
Proverb"The early bird catches the worm" — those who begin early have an advantage over others.
🛋️ Couch Potato ↔ 🐝 Busy Bee
IdiomMeaningOpposite
*Couch potatoA lazy person who sits around all day, usually watching TVBusy bee
*Busy beeAn energetic, hardworking person who is always doing somethingCouch potato
🦋 Social Butterfly ↔ 🐚 Shrinking Violet
IdiomMeaningOpposite
*Social butterflyA very sociable, outgoing person who moves easily between social groupsShrinking violet
*HomebodySomeone who prefers to stay at home rather than socializeSocial butterfly
*Wet Blanket / Party PooperSomeone who dampens the fun at social events.Social butterfly
🐺 Lone Wolf ↔ 🤝 Team Player
IdiomMeaningOpposite
*Lone wolfSomeone who prefers to work or live independently, without relying on othersTeam player
Team playerSomeone who works well with others and thrives as part of a groupLone wolf
📚 Bookworm ↔ 💬 Chatterbox
IdiomMeaningOpposite / Contrast
BookwormSomeone who loves reading and spends a lot of time studying books—
*ChatterboxA person who talks constantly and excessivelyA man/woman of few words
*A man/woman of few wordsSomeone who speaks rarely and says only what is necessaryChatterbox
More Key Idioms
IdiomMeaning
*Bed bug / SleepyheadSomeone who loves sleeping in and is reluctant to get out of bed
Dark horseA person whose abilities are unknown, who then surprisingly succeeds
A cold fishAn unfriendly, emotionally distant person who is hard to warm to
A walking encyclopediaA person who knows a great deal about many different subjects
A fish out of waterSomeone who feels awkward and out of place in their current situation
A people personSomeone who genuinely enjoys the company of others and relates to them easily
A stick-in-the-mudA dull, unadventurous person who resists any change or new ideas
A go-getterAn ambitious, energetic person who pursues goals with drive and determination
Key Points for the Exam
✦ Remember
  • Always look at the context — the sentence around the idiom tells you if it is positive or negative.
  • Know your opposite pairs: night owl/early bird, social butterfly/shrinking violet, lone wolf/team player, couch potato/busy bee, chatterbox/man of few words.
  • Never interpret these idioms literally — a "bookworm" is not a worm, and a "dark horse" is not a horse.
  • These idioms can appear in MCQs (what does it mean?) or fill-in-the-blank (complete the sentence with the correct idiom).

Roald Dahl — Boy
About the Book

Boy: Tales of Childhood (1984) is Roald Dahl's autobiography about his early years — birth through boarding school. Written with dark humour and vivid detail. Separate from his adult memoir Going Solo.

Key Facts at a Glance
DetailAnswer
GenreAutobiography / memoir
ToneNostalgic, darkly comic, occasionally critical
BornLlandaff, Cardiff, Wales — to Norwegian parents
FatherHarald Dahl — a prosperous ship broker
SchoolSt. Peter's preparatory school, Weston-super-Mare
SummersSpent in Norway — family maintained strong cultural ties
School lifeMarked by strict discipline and corporal punishment
Mrs. Pratchett

Owner of the sweet shop near school. Small, dirty, bad-tempered — she handled sweets with unwashed hands and retrieved dropped sweets from the floor to resell. Her unpleasantness directly triggers the boys' revenge plot.

The Great Mouse Plot — Summary

Dahl and friends find a dead mouse. Driven by hatred of Mrs. Pratchett, one boy distracts her at the counter while another drops the mouse into her gobstopper jar. She identifies them; the headmaster canes them as punishment. Dahl recounts the caning with barely concealed anger.

Themes & Techniques
Theme / TechniqueHow It Appears
Adult cruelty & authorityThe caning; rigid school rules and punishment culture
Childhood imagination & rebellionThe Mouse Plot — children resisting unjust adult power
Identity & heritageDahl's Norwegian roots; summer trips to Norway
Humour & exaggerationEven painful events told with dark comedy and irony
Child's perspectiveTitle "Boy" signals events seen through a child's eyes, not reinterpreted by adult wisdom

Email Writing
Email Structure

A formal email follows five structural pillars — memorise the order.

Gmail
Request for Assignment Extension
From: student@university.edu
To: jiqbal.dell@riphah.edu.pk
BCC:
CC:
Date: Mon, 31 Mar 2025, 10:14 AM

① Salutation

Dear Sir. Javed Iqbal,

② Purpose Line

I hope this email finds you well. I am writing to request an extension on the assignment due on 15th April, as I was unfortunately unable to complete it on time due to a family emergency last week.

③ Main Body

The situation arose suddenly and required my full attention for several days. I have since made significant progress on the work and am confident I can submit it to a high standard with a small amount of additional time. I would be most grateful if you could grant me an extension of five working days — a revised deadline of 20th April.

④ Closing Sentence

I apologise for any inconvenience this may cause, and I look forward to your response at your earliest convenience.

⑤ Sign-Off

Sincerely Yours,
Hanan Shafay

Tone — Formal vs Informal
AvoidUse Instead
Hi / HeyDear [Name],
Thanks!Thank you for your time.
I wanna / gonnaI would like to / I intend to
ASAPat your earliest convenience
Yeah / NopeYes / Unfortunately,
I need you to…I would be grateful if you could…
Subject Line Tips
  • Weak "Question" / "Hello"
  • Strong "Request for Assignment Extension — Student ID 4821"

PurposePhrase
OpeningI am writing to bring to your attention… / I am writing with reference to…
RequestI would be most grateful if you could… / I respectfully request that…
ClosingI look forward to your response. / I await your reply with interest.
