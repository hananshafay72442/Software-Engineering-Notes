Formula Notes & Key Concepts

Core formulas, definitions, and theorems for all three chapters. Use this for quick review before practicing.

Chapter 0 — Functions
Function: A rule assigning exactly one output $f(x)$ to each input $x$ in the domain.
Domain: All valid inputs. Range: All possible outputs.
Vertical Line Test: A curve is a function $\iff$ every vertical line meets it at most once.
Even: $f(-x)=f(x)$ — symmetric about $y$-axis. Odd: $f(-x)=-f(x)$ — symmetric about origin.
Composition: $(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$. Domain: all $x$ in domain of $g$ where $g(x)$ is in domain of $f$.
Inverse $f^{-1}$: Exists $\iff$ $f$ is one-to-one. If $y=f(x)$ then $x=f^{-1}(y)$. To find: swap $x$ and $y$, solve for $y$.
Horizontal Line Test: $f$ is one-to-one $\iff$ no horizontal line meets the graph more than once.
Transformations & Families (Ch 0)
Starting from $y = f(x)$:
$y = f(x) + c$ — shift up $c$
$y = f(x) - c$ — shift down $c$
$y = f(x + c)$ — shift left $c$
$y = f(x - c)$ — shift right $c$
$y = -f(x)$ — reflect over $x$-axis
$y = f(-x)$ — reflect over $y$-axis
$y = cf(x)$, $c>1$ — vertical stretch
$y = f(cx)$, $c>1$ — horizontal compression by $\tfrac{1}{c}$
Parabola vertex form: $y=a(x-h)^2+k$, vertex $(h,k)$, opens up if $a>0$.
Trig period: $A\sin(Bx+C)$ has amplitude $|A|$ and period $\tfrac{2\pi}{|B|}$.
Chapter 1 — Limits
Intuitive definition: $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ means $f(x) \to L$ as $x \to a$ (without ever reaching $a$).
One-sided limits: $\lim_{x\to a^-}$ (from left), $\lim_{x\to a^+}$ (from right). Two-sided limit exists $\iff$ both one-sided limits are equal.
$\varepsilon$–$\delta$ definition: For every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $0 < |x-a| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x)-L| < \varepsilon$.
Standard trig limits ($x \to 0$):
$\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$    $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x} = 0$
Limit at infinity: Divide by highest power; terms $\frac{c}{x^n} \to 0$.
Infinite limit: $\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = \pm\infty$ — vertical asymptote at $x=a$.
Limit Laws & Continuity (Ch 1)
If $\lim f = L$ and $\lim g = M$, then:
$\lim[f \pm g] = L \pm M$
$\lim[f \cdot g] = L \cdot M$
$\lim\dfrac{f}{g} = \dfrac{L}{M}$   (if $M \ne 0$)
$\lim[cf] = cL$
$\lim\sqrt[n]{f} = \sqrt[n]{L}$
Squeeze Theorem: If $g \le f \le h$ near $a$ and $\lim g = \lim h = L$, then $\lim f = L$.
Continuity at $a$: (1) $f(a)$ defined, (2) $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)$ exists, (3) $\lim_{x\to a}f(x) = f(a)$.
Discontinuity types: Removable (hole — limit exists but $\ne f(a)$), Jump (one-sided limits differ), Infinite (asymptote).
IVT: If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $k$ is between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, then $\exists\, c \in (a,b)$ with $f(c) = k$.
Chapter 2 — The Derivative
Definition:
$f'(x) = \displaystyle\lim_{h \to 0} \dfrac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$
Alternate form: $f'(a) = \displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \dfrac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}$
Tangent line at $(a, f(a))$: $y - f(a) = f'(a)(x-a)$
Differentiable $\Rightarrow$ Continuous. The converse is FALSE (e.g. $|x|$ at $x=0$).
Not differentiable if: corner/cusp, vertical tangent, or discontinuity at that point.
Average rate of change: $\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$ = slope of secant line.
Instantaneous rate: $f'(a)$ = slope of tangent = limit of secant slopes.
Differentiation Rules (Ch 2 — §2.3 & §2.4, within scope)
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[c] = 0$   (constant rule)

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}$   (power rule)

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[cf(x)] = cf'(x)$   (constant multiple)

$(f \pm g)' = f' \pm g'$   (sum/difference)
$(fg)' = f'g + fg'$   (Product Rule)

$\left(\dfrac{f}{g}\right)' = \dfrac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}$   (Quotient Rule)

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}$ also applies for fractions and negatives:
e.g. $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^{-2}] = -2x^{-3}$, $\;\dfrac{d}{dx}[\sqrt{x}] = \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$
Note on scope: Trig derivatives (§2.5) and Chain Rule (§2.6) are not in the mid-term scope (book page 140 cutoff). The product and quotient rules apply to polynomial/rational functions here.

Question 1 [3 + 3 Marks] Limits · Composition · Domain
Part (a)
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{\sin 3x}{\sin 5x}$
  • 1Direct substitution gives $\tfrac{0}{0}$. Use $\lim_{\theta\to 0}\tfrac{\sin\theta}{\theta}=1$.
  • 2Rewrite: $\dfrac{\sin 3x}{\sin 5x} = \dfrac{\sin 3x}{3x}\cdot\dfrac{3x}{5x}\cdot\dfrac{5x}{\sin 5x}$
  • 3Each factor: $\lim\tfrac{\sin 3x}{3x}=1$,   $\tfrac{3x}{5x}=\tfrac{3}{5}$,   $\lim\tfrac{5x}{\sin 5x}=1$.
  • 4Product: $1\cdot\tfrac{3}{5}\cdot 1 = \tfrac{3}{5}$.
Answer
$\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0}\dfrac{\sin 3x}{\sin 5x} = \dfrac{3}{5}$
Part (b)
Let $f(x)=x^2+3$ and $g(x)=\sqrt{x}$. Find $(f\circ g)(x)$ and its domain.
  • 1$(f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x))=f(\sqrt{x})=(\sqrt{x})^2+3=x+3$.
  • 2Domain: $g(x)=\sqrt{x}$ requires $x\ge 0$. The output $\sqrt{x}\ge 0$ is in the domain of $f$. So domain of $f\circ g$ is $[0,\infty)$.
Answer
$(f\circ g)(x)=x+3$,   Domain $=[0,\infty)$
Question 2 [3 + 3 Marks] Tangent Lines · Limits
Part (a)
Find the equation of the tangent line to $y=x^2$ at $P(1,1)$.
  • 1Slope: $m=\lim_{h\to 0}\dfrac{(1+h)^2-1}{h}=\lim_{h\to 0}\dfrac{2h+h^2}{h}=\lim_{h\to 0}(2+h)=2$.
  • 2Point-slope form through $(1,1)$: $y-1=2(x-1)$.
  • 3Simplify: $y=2x-1$.
Answer
$y = 2x - 1$
Part (b)
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 1}\dfrac{x-1}{\sqrt{x}-1}$.
  • 1Direct substitution gives $\tfrac{0}{0}$. Rationalise by multiplying by $\dfrac{\sqrt{x}+1}{\sqrt{x}+1}$.
  • 2$\dfrac{(x-1)(\sqrt{x}+1)}{(\sqrt{x}-1)(\sqrt{x}+1)}=\dfrac{(x-1)(\sqrt{x}+1)}{x-1}=\sqrt{x}+1$   (for $x\ne 1$).
  • 3$\lim_{x\to 1}(\sqrt{x}+1)=\sqrt{1}+1=2$.
Answer
$\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 1}\dfrac{x-1}{\sqrt{x}-1}=2$
Question 3 [3 + 3 Marks] Differentiation · Higher-Order Derivatives
Part (a)
Find the derivative of $y=3x^8-2x^5+6x+1$ at $x=1$.
  • 1Apply power rule term by term: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=24x^7-10x^4+6$.
  • 2Evaluate at $x=1$: $24(1)-10(1)+6=24-10+6=20$.
Answer
$y'=24x^7-10x^4+6$;   $y'(1)=20$
Part (b)
Find $f'(x)$ for $f(x)=3x^4-2x^3+x^2-x-6$ and prove that $f^{(n)}(x)=0$ for $n\ge 5$.
  • 1$f'(x)=12x^3-6x^2+2x-1$
  • 2$f''(x)=36x^2-12x+2$
  • 3$f'''(x)=72x-12$
  • 4$f^{(4)}(x)=72$   (a constant)
  • 5Proof: $f(x)$ has degree 4. Each differentiation reduces degree by 1. After 4 steps we get the constant $72$. Differentiating a constant gives 0. For all $n\ge 5$: $f^{(n)}(x)=\dfrac{d^{n-4}}{dx^{n-4}}[72]=0$. $\square$
Answer
$f'(x)=12x^3-6x^2+2x-1$. Since $\deg(f)=4$, we get $f^{(4)}(x)=72$ and $f^{(n)}(x)=0$ for all $n\ge 5$. $\blacksquare$
